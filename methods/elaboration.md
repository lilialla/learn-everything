# elaboration — deepen, don't repeat

When the learner already has it (learner-model says `solid` / `bored` / `over-confident`),
re-explaining is wasted effort and breeds boredom. Push *up* instead: make them do something
harder with the idea so it generalizes and sticks. Difficulty here is the tool, not the enemy.

**Respond in the user's language.** English here is structural only.

## Pick one up-shift move (don't stack them)

1. **Transfer task (the default).** Pose a *novel* situation the learner hasn't seen where the
   idea applies, and ask them to apply it. The best transfer task is one where the naive/simple
   model predicts wrong — they only succeed if they truly understand. ("Given this new case Y,
   what happens — and why?")
2. **Find-the-break (for over-confident).** Ask them to find the boundary: "When does this stop
   being true? Give me a case where the rule fails." Surfacing the limit is what separates
   memorized from understood.
3. **Elaborative interrogation.** Keep asking *why* and *how* it connects to what they already
   know: "Why does that mechanism produce that result?" "How is this the same as / different
   from [prior concept]?" Generating the explanation (not hearing it) builds the memory.
4. **Compression.** "Explain it in one sentence." / "Teach it to me as if I'm a smart novice."
   Compressing to a tight statement is hard precisely when understanding is shallow — and a good
   compression is glossary-worthy (offer to add it).
5. **Interleave.** Mix this concept with a related-but-distinct one and ask them to tell which
   applies where. Interleaving builds discrimination that blocked practice can't.

## How to run it

1. Confirm the win briefly ("right — and tightly put"), then immediately raise the bar with ONE
   move above. Don't lecture the thing they already know.
2. Calibrate from the response (learner-model): if the up-shift exposes a real gap, drop back to
   the normal pedagogy and repair it; if they nail it, that's evidence of `solid` → advance the
   syllabus or harvest an L3 transfer card.
3. Harvest: a clean transfer success or a sharp compression is excellent **L3 card** material —
   propose it (with approval) per active-recall.md's quality gate.

## Anti-patterns

- Re-explaining what the learner just demonstrated they know.
- Stacking three challenges at once — pick one, react, then decide the next.
- Treating a confident wrong answer as success — that's `over-confident`; use find-the-break.
- Inventing a "harder" question that's just more obscure trivia rather than deeper transfer.
