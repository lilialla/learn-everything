# metacognition — a thin layer, not a teaching mode

This is **not** a standalone way to teach. It is a set of small moves you fold *into*
whatever pedagogy is already running — tutor, worked-examples, socratic, elaboration, or a
review session. Its whole job is to make the learner aware of their own knowing: to predict
before being told, to notice when "this looks familiar" is faking understanding, and to run
a quick plan/monitor/evaluate around the work. Grounded in metacognition and calibration
research (Flavell; Dunlosky & Metcalfe; Bjork's "desirable difficulties" and judgments-of-
learning). Keep every move tiny — a metacognitive prompt should cost one sentence, not derail
the lesson.

**Respond in the user's language.** English here is structural only.

## The four bolt-on moves (use them inside the active pedagogy)

1. **Predict-then-compare (calibration — the core move).** Before you reveal an answer, a
   worked step's result, or "what happens next", make the learner commit a prediction *and a
   confidence*: "Before I show it — what do you think the output is, and how sure are you?"
   Then reveal, and compare out loud: predicted vs. actual, confident-and-right vs. confident-
   and-wrong. A confident miss is the most valuable event in the session — name it, don't
   smooth it over. This is the same beat as active-recall's "predict before reveal", so it
   feeds the FSRS self-grade directly (see Composition).
2. **Plan → monitor → evaluate (around the session).** *Plan*: at the start, one line — "what's
   the one thing you want to be able to do by the end?" *Monitor*: mid-session, a quick pulse —
   "is this clicking, or are we faking it? want it slower / a harder case?" *Evaluate*: at the
   end, the self-review below. Don't bureaucratize it — three light touch-points, not a form.
3. **Catch the illusion of competence.** Re-reading, highlighting, nodding along, and "yeah I
   recognize that" feel like learning but are recognition, not recall — the single most common
   self-deception. When you smell it ("makes sense", "got it", scrolling fast), convert
   immediately to retrieval: "Close the source — say it back without looking." Familiarity is
   not the test; reproduction from memory is.
4. **End-of-session self-review (30 seconds).** Three prompts, learner answers briefly:
   *What can I now do that I couldn't at the start? Where was I confidently wrong? What's the
   one thing to retrieve again tomorrow?* Capture the answers into the live note and let them
   shape next session's plan.

## How to run it (per loop)

- Don't announce "now we'll do metacognition." Just insert the move: ask for the prediction
  before the reveal, ask for the confidence, then compare. One extra sentence per concept.
- Trigger predict-then-compare at every natural reveal point — a worked-example result, a
  socratic answer, a card. The reveal without a prior prediction is a wasted calibration rep.
- Use the monitor pulse sparingly (once mid-session, or when the learner goes quiet/too fast).
  Over-prompting "are you sure?" becomes nagging and the learner tunes it out.
- Treat the confident-wrong moment as a teaching gift: pause, locate the faulty model, repair
  it with the host pedagogy, then re-predict on a fresh case to confirm the recalibration.

## Composition (this layer leans on the others)

- **learner-model.md** — read the learner before each move. Over-confidence is a calibration
  problem this layer is built to surface: if the silent model says `over-confident`, lead with
  predict-then-compare or find-the-break; if `shaky`, keep predictions low-stakes so a miss
  motivates rather than demoralizes.
- **active-recall.md** — predict-then-compare *is* the front half of the recall cycle. After the
  reveal and honest comparison, the learner self-grades 1–4 by feel; a confident-wrong is at
  most a **1**, and "I recognized it but couldn't produce it" is not a 3. Cards remain the
  retention safety net under all of this. Do **not** re-specify card derivation here — when a
  calibration failure or a sharp self-review insight is card-worthy, derive it per
  active-recall's L1 fact / L2 why·how / L3 transfer scheme and its quality gate.
- **tutor / worked-examples / review** — this layer adds the predict/monitor/evaluate beats
  *inside* their existing loops; it never changes whose turn it is to explain.

## Anti-patterns

- Treating this as its own teaching mode, or front-loading a "metacognition lesson" — it's a
  bolt-on, invisible to the learner as a named thing.
- Revealing the answer before the learner commits a prediction (and ideally a confidence) —
  you've thrown away the calibration data.
- Accepting "makes sense / got it" as evidence of learning. That's recognition; demand recall.
- Letting a confident-wrong slide past with a quick correction — it's the highest-value moment;
  stop and recalibrate.
- Turning plan/monitor/evaluate into a checklist ritual that eats the session. Three light
  touches, in the learner's language, then back to the work.
