# deliberate-practice — build the skill, rep by rep

Use this when the goal is *doing*, not *knowing*: solving integrals fast, writing clean
functions, sight-reading chords, framing an argument, conjugating verbs on demand. Teaching
(tutor) installs the idea; this drills the idea into a reliable skill. It is not spaced review
(active-recall) — that maintains a skill you already have; this *manufactures* one by repeated
effortful reps at the edge of current ability, each with immediate correction. The research is
Ericsson's deliberate practice: gains come not from time-on-task but from focused reps just
beyond comfort with tight feedback loops.

**Respond in the user's language.** English here is structural only.

## Before drilling: find the edge and name the target

1. **Isolate ONE sub-skill.** A skill is a stack; don't drill the whole thing. Find the
   specific sub-skill that's the current bottleneck — the one where performance breaks down.
   "Get better at French" → drill *just* past-tense conjugation of irregular verbs. (Use
   learner-model to locate the edge: where do they actually fail, not where the syllabus says.)
2. **Set a concrete, measurable target.** Vague goals can't drive feedback. Make it checkable:
   "factor these 10 quadratics, ≥8 correct, under 6 min" — not "get good at factoring."
   The target defines what counts as a hit and a miss, which is what makes feedback possible.
3. **Pitch difficulty at the edge.** Reps should be *uncomfortable but reachable* — roughly
   ~80% success. Too easy = no learning (that's just review); too hard = flailing without a
   clean signal. Adjust the rep, not the target, to hit that zone.

## The drill loop (run for EACH rep)

1. **Pose one rep.** A single, narrow, doable instance of the sub-skill. Short by design.
2. **Learner attempts** — they produce the answer / move / output themselves. No watching you
   do it; the rep is theirs.
3. **Immediate corrective feedback.** Right away, mark hit or miss against the target, and on a
   miss say *exactly* what was wrong and the one thing to fix next time — specific and short,
   not a re-lecture. Immediacy is the active ingredient; delayed feedback wastes the rep.
4. **Adjust the next rep.** Three hits in a row → nudge difficulty up (push past comfort).
   A miss → hold or shrink: re-rep the *same* failure mode until it's clean (consolidate before
   advancing). Don't move the edge faster than the learner closes it.
5. **Log the sticking sub-skill.** Whatever keeps missing is the real target — track it across
   reps and sessions so practice concentrates there, not on what they already nail.

## Push then consolidate

Alternate the two phases deliberately. **Push**: once reps land reliably, raise difficulty so
they're working at the edge again — comfort means the skill stopped growing. **Consolidate**:
after a hard push, do a short block at the just-mastered level so the gain stabilizes before
the next push. A session that only pushes burns out; one that only consolidates plateaus.

## How it differs from FSRS review — and when to choose it

- **active-recall (FSRS)** maintains *retention* of a known item on a forgetting schedule: one
  attempt, self-grade, long gaps. **deliberate-practice** builds *capability* in one sitting:
  many reps, external correction, edge difficulty, no schedule.
- Choose **deliberate-practice** when the learner *understands but can't yet reliably perform*
  (slow, error-prone, effortful execution). Choose **active-recall** when they *could perform
  but might forget over time*. Choose **tutor** when they don't yet understand the idea at all.
- The handoff: once a sub-skill is reliably hit at target, it stops needing reps and starts
  needing *maintenance* — graduate it to active-recall.

## Composing with the other methods

- **learner-model.md** — read the learner every rep to keep difficulty at the edge: a clean
  hit upgrades grasp and raises the bar; a confident miss is `over-confident` (the move was
  wrong *and* they didn't notice) — slow down and target that failure mode directly.
- **active-recall.md** — the recurring miss is the single source for card derivation: turn a
  persistent sticking point into a card per its quality gate (L1 the fact behind the error,
  L2 the why/how it goes wrong, L3 a transfer rep), reviewed on the FSRS schedule so the
  hard-won fix doesn't decay. Don't re-specify card levels here — defer to that gate. Cards
  remain the retention safety net under the skill you just built.

## Anti-patterns

- Drilling the whole skill at once instead of isolating the one weak sub-skill.
- A fuzzy target ("get better") — without a hit/miss line there is no real feedback.
- Delayed or vague feedback ("nice try, keep going") — it must be immediate and specific.
- Staying in the comfort zone: easy reps the learner already passes are review, not practice.
- Advancing on a single lucky hit instead of consolidating the failure mode first.
- Turning a miss into a lecture — give the one corrective cue, then pose the next rep.
